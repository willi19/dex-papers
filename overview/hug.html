HUG: Human Universal Grasping

arXiv 2026New York University (with Tsinghua University and University of Michigan)flow-matchinghuman-dataMANO-retargetingdexterous-grasping

HUG is a dexterous grasping framework that learns entirely from human grasp data rather than robot demonstrations or simulation. It collects 1M egocentric image-grasp pairs (1M-HUGs) from Aria smart glasses across 6,707 recordings and 41 buildings, then trains a point-conditioned flow-matching transformer to generate diverse human grasps for a user-clicked object in a single RGB-D image. The model predicts MANO hand pose parameters (a 99-dimensional grasp state: wrist translation, 6D wrist rotation, and finger joint articulation), fusing DINOv2 RGB features with PointNeXt point-cloud features via point painting. Predicted MANO grasps are then retargeted to different anthropomorphic robot hands without per-embodiment retraining, enabling zero-shot deployment.

Humans are the most natural and scalable source of grasping data, so HUG trains purely on human egocentric video instead of robot or sim data. A flow-matching transformer generates diverse grasps conditioned on RGB-D plus a 2D pixel click lifted to a 3D metric query point on the target object. Architecture fuses DINOv2-Base RGB features with PointNeXt point-cloud features via 'point painting' (projecting centroids onto the RGB image to sample patch features). The model predicts MANO hand-pose parameters with a fixed canonical hand shape for consistency between training and deployment. Predicted MANO grasps retarget to multiple robot hands (e.g., Ability Hand, WUJI Hand) without per-hand retraining, giving cross-embodiment transfer. Deployment uses a minimal open-loop setup, demonstrating the learned grasps transfer to unseen objects and unseen homes.

1M-HUGs: a dataset of 1M egocentric image-grasp pairs of natural human grasps spanning 6,707 recordings across 41 buildings. HUG: a point-conditioned flow-matching model that predicts MANO grasps from RGB-D and retargets to multiple robot embodiments; described as the first grasping framework trained purely on human data and deployable across multiple robot hands. HUG-Bench: 90 unseen objects with metric-scale 3D meshes across 5 geometric categories for paired simulation and real-world evaluation (30 test / 60 validation). Real-world tabletop: 66.7% success vs Dex1B 43.7% and CAP 32.7% (+23% and +34% over baselines). Real-world in-the-wild: 62.0% success on unseen objects in unseen homes with no onsite tuning. Simulation on HUG-Bench (30-object test): 73.0% +/- 2.6 success, approaching the human-grasp oracle at 94.0% +/- 1.4.

Demos show a robot hand autonomously lifting tabletop objects (30 objects, 10 trials each) and grasping unseen objects in unseen homes in the wild, with side-by-side comparisons against Dex1B and CAP baselines.
