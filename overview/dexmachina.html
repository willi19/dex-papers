DexMachina: Functional Retargeting for Bimanual Dexterous Manipulation

ICML 2026 (arXiv 2505.24853, May 2025)Stanford University, NVIDIARLbimanualdexteroushuman-video

DexMachina tackles 'functional retargeting': learning dexterous manipulation policies that track object states from human hand-object demonstrations, rather than mimicking hand joints directly. It targets long-horizon, bimanual tasks with articulated objects, which are hard due to large action spaces, spatiotemporal discontinuities, and the embodiment gap between human and robot hands. The core idea is a curriculum that uses virtual object controllers whose strength decays over training: the object is first driven automatically toward its target states so the policy can gradually take over under motion and contact guidance. The authors also release a simulation benchmark of articulated-object tasks and multiple robot hands for functional hardware comparison.

Functional retargeting: learn to reproduce object trajectories/states from human demonstrations instead of directly copying human hand poses, sidestepping the embodiment gap. Virtual object controllers with decaying strength: external controllers initially drive the object toward its target states, then weaken so the policy must take over. Curriculum-based RL that provides motion and contact guidance, easing learning on long-horizon bimanual articulated-object tasks. Demonstrations sourced from the ARCTIC dataset of human hand-object interactions with articulated objects. A simulation benchmark with curated hand assets and articulated objects enabling functional comparison across hardware designs. Finding that degrees of freedom matters more than hand size for manipulation performance.

A novel curriculum-based RL algorithm for functional retargeting using decaying-strength virtual object controllers. A simulation benchmark with 5 articulated objects (from ARCTIC) and 6 open-source dexterous hands (Inspire, Allegro, XHand, Schunk, Ability, DexRobot/DexHand), spanning 7 demonstration clips of long- and short-horizon motions. State-of-the-art results: consistently best performance across all hands and tasks on long-horizon demos, and roughly 70 to 90 percent success on short-horizon tasks (AUC-ADD metric). Significant improvement over baselines including kinematic retargeting (insufficient), an ObjDex task-reward reimplementation, task+auxiliary rewards without curriculum, and a ManipTrans-style curriculum (training instability). Demonstration that hand degrees of freedom is more important than hand size for functional performance, enabling functional comparison of hardware designs.

Given human hand-object demonstrations of bimanual articulated-object tasks, DexMachina trains robot-hand policies that reproduce the demonstrated object motion. It works across multiple robot hand designs and serves as a benchmark for comparing dexterous hardware.
