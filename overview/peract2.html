PerAct2: Benchmarking and Learning for Robotic Bimanual Manipulation Tasks

arXiv 2407.00278 (cs.RO), 2024University of Washington, Karlsruhe Institute of Technology (KIT), NVIDIAimitation-learningbehavior-cloninglanguage-conditionedbimanual

PerAct2 is both a benchmark and a learning method for two-arm robotic manipulation. It extends the RLBench benchmark to bimanual settings, adding 13 new tasks with 23 unique task variations that require coordination and adaptability between arms. The PerAct2 agent is a language-conditioned behavioral cloning policy that integrates language understanding with action prediction to perform coordinated bimanual tasks from user-specified goals. It builds on the original PerAct (Perceiver-Actor) voxel-based transformer framework, adapting it to predict actions for two arms simultaneously.

Uses a single PerceiverIO transformer backbone that ingests 3D voxel grids built from RGB-D images, proprioception, and a language goal, rather than duplicating the representation per arm. Splits the latent space between the two arms and uses combined self-attention to enable implicit coordination and communication between arms. Outputs discretized 6-DoF end-effector poses, gripper open/close states, and collision-avoidance flags for each arm. Tasks are categorized by coordination type (e.g., temporal/spatial coupling, synchronization, physical coupling) and span prehensile, non-prehensile, and mixed manipulation. The agent is robot-agnostic and was demonstrated in the real world on the ARMAR-6 humanoid.

Introduces a bimanual extension of RLBench with 13 new tasks and 23 unique task variations requiring two-arm coordination. PerAct2 achieves a 16.8% average task success rate trained on 100 demonstrations. Reports comparisons against baselines including ACT, RVT-LF (10.5%), and PerAct-LF (17.5%) on individual tasks. Trains more efficiently than the leader-follower baseline: 54 hours for PerAct2 vs. 89 hours for PerAct-LF. Releases code, benchmark, and datasets (128x128 and 256x256 image sizes with train/val/test splits) publicly.

Given a natural-language goal and RGB-D observations, it predicts coordinated 6-DoF actions for two robot arms to perform bimanual manipulation tasks such as handovers, lifting, pushing, and drawer operations.
