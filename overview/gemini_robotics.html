Gemini Robotics: Bringing AI into the Physical World

2025Google DeepMindVLAgeneralistreasoning

Gemini Robotics is a family of AI models from the Google DeepMind Gemini Robotics Team (arXiv:2503.20020, March 2025), built on the Gemini 2.0 foundation, that brings multimodal foundation-model capabilities into physical robot control. It comprises two models: Gemini Robotics-ER (Embodied Reasoning), a vision-language model that extends Gemini's multimodal reasoning with enhanced spatial and temporal understanding, and Gemini Robotics, a vision-language-action (VLA) model that directly outputs robot actions for dexterous manipulation. The VLA model uses a cloud-hosted distilled backbone (latency optimized to ~160ms) plus an onboard local action decoder, achieving ~250ms end-to-end latency and an effective 50Hz control frequency. The work is positioned as a substantial step toward general-purpose robots that can act safely and competently in the physical world.

Two-tier model family: Gemini Robotics-ER provides embodied reasoning (2D detection, pointing, trajectory and grasp prediction, 3D bounding boxes, multi-view correspondence), while Gemini Robotics is a VLA model that emits robot actions directly; both are built on Gemini 2.0. Embodied reasoning enables robot control without action-trained weights: zero-shot control via code generation against robot APIs, and few-shot control via in-context learning over tokenized example trajectories. The VLA architecture splits computation between a cloud-hosted distilled backbone (~160ms) and an onboard local action decoder, yielding ~250ms end-to-end latency and a 50Hz effective control rate for smooth, reactive movements. Training uses thousands of hours of expert teleoperation demonstrations collected on ALOHA 2 robots over roughly 12 months, combined with diverse multimodal data (web documents, code, video, and QA data) inherited from Gemini. Specialization pathways extend the base model: long-horizon dexterous fine-tuning (about 2000-5000 episodes per task), reasoning-enhanced variants via trajectory re-labeling, few-shot adaptation from limited demonstrations, and transfer to new robot embodiments. The team introduces ERQA, a 400-question open-source benchmark for embodied reasoning covering spatial reasoning, trajectory reasoning, action reasoning, state estimation, pointing, multi-view reasoning, and task reasoning.

On the ERQA embodied-reasoning benchmark, Gemini 2.0 Pro Experimental reaches 48.3% (no chain-of-thought) and 54.8% (with CoT), and Gemini 2.0 Flash 46.3%, outperforming GPT-4o (47.0%) and Claude 3.5 Sonnet (35.5%). Gemini Robotics-ER leads on 2D pointing benchmarks: Paco-LVIS 71.3% (vs Molmo 47.1%), Pixmo-Point 49.5% (vs Molmo 12.5%), Where2Place 45.0%; and on 3D detection (SUN-RGBD AP@15) reaches 48.3%, beating specialized baselines ImVoxelNet (43.7%) and Implicit3D (24.1%). For control via reasoning alone, Gemini Robotics-ER achieves 53% average zero-shot success on ALOHA 2 simulation (50 trials) vs 27% for Gemini 2.0 Flash, and 65% success in both sim and real-world with few-shot in-context learning (10 demonstrations). On long-horizon dexterous tasks (20 trials), lunch-box packing reaches 100% success; the model also performs origami-fox folding and a spelling board game (67% accuracy on unseen hand-drawn sketches). Fast adaptation with at most 100 demonstrations yields over 70% success on 7 of 8 new tasks, with 2 tasks reaching 100%; the blog states the model can learn new tasks from as few as 100 demonstrations. Cross-embodiment transfer to a bi-arm Franka platform reaches 63% average success on in-distribution tasks and runs on the Apollo humanoid; the blog claims Gemini Robotics more than doubles performance on a comprehensive generalization benchmark versus competing VLAs and gives 2x-3x end-to-end success over base Gemini 2.0.

In figures and project-page videos, the robots (ALOHA 2 bi-arm, a bi-arm Franka platform, and the Apollo humanoid) perform dexterous, multi-step manipulation such as folding an origami fox, packing a lunch-box/snacks into Ziploc bags, playing a spelling board game and a card game, and adding food to a salad with tongs or a spoon, as well as short tasks like opening an eyeglasses case, pouring, and wrapping a headphone wire. The demos emphasize reactive behavior, including continuous environmental monitoring and dynamic re-planning when objects slip or the scene changes mid-task, and generalization across visual, instruction (including multilingual and typo'd prompts), and action variations.
